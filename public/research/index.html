Research

Our primary goal is to separate the neuronal mechanisms for positive and negative reinforcement and determine how these processes are recruited and transformed throughout the development and expression of substance use disorder (SUD). We focus on the signaling and function of subpopulations in the lateral hypothalamus (LHA) and their regulation of dopamine (DA) signaling during reinforcement behavior. Our long-term aim is to identify interventions that can selectively modulate positive and negative reinforcement for the treatment of SUD.

01

LHA and striatal dopamine encoding in positive and negative reinforcement

The LHA contains GABA and glutamate neurons that drive opposing reinforcement phenotypes and regulate dopamine in the ventral striatum, but their role during positive and negative reinforcement remains poorly understood. We determine the dynamics of tracked neurons using 2-photon calcium imaging with microprisms and their relation to downstream DA signaling using multi-site fiber photometry. We then test the causal role of these circuits using chemogenetics and disconnection approaches. These projects establish a framework for how the LHA regulates the DA system to mediate reinforcement—mechanisms that may be recruited during the development of SUD.

02

LHA and striatal dopamine during development of motivation for opioids

The role of DA in SUD is well established, but the spatiotemporal DA dynamics during opioid self-administration and the role of descending LHA regulation of the DA system in SUD remain poorly understood. We utilize the imaging and behavioral approaches developed in Project 1 to determine how this circuit regulates motivation for opioids across the continuum from initial use to compulsive seeking. Understanding these longitudinal circuit changes may reveal intervention points for treating opioid use disorder.

03

Linking activity-defined clusters of LHA neurons to circuit and behavior

It is unknown whether discrete clusters of LHA neurons selectively modulate subcomponents of behavior and DA release in subregions of the striatum. We use 2-photon calcium imaging during reinforcement behavior to identify clusters of cells with unique activity dynamics, then assess the behavioral function of these cells using holographic stimulation during behavior. We also measure the relationship between these cell clusters and downstream DA signaling by multiplexing 2-photon imaging and multi-site fiber photometry.
