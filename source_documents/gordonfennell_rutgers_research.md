Dissecting neuronal substrates of positive and negative reinforcement in health and disease

     My  primary  research  goal  is  to  separate  the  neuronal  mechanisms  for
positive and negative reinforcement and determine how these processes are
recruited  and  transformed  throughout  the  development  and  expression  of
substance use disorder (SUD). My first projects will focus on the signaling and
function  of  subpopulations  in  the  lateral  hypothalamus  (LHA)  and  their
regulation  of  dopamine  (DA)  signaling  during  positive,  negative,  and  opioid
reinforcement. My long-term goal is to identify interventions that can selectively
modulate positive and negative reinforcement for the treatment of SUD.
Background and Vision The development of SUD is characterized in part by
a  transition  from  positive  reinforcement  to  negative  reinforcement1,  but  the
neuronal  circuits  that  mediate  these  forms  of  reinforcement  remain  unclear.
Multiple avenues of research suggest the LHA shape motivation for opioids as
the LHA cells express a diversity of opioid related markers2–4 and plays a role
in  opioid  withdrawal5–7.  The  LHA  is  deeply  interconnected  with  the  midbrain
dopamine  neurons  that  project  throughout  the  brain8.  Through  engaging  DA  release  at  specific  downstream
sites, the LHA is positioned to modulate motivation for opioids through multiple mechanisms, but the causal role
of the LHA and its regulation of the DA system in motivating opioid self-administration remains poorly understood.
     My  lab  will  address  these  questions  using  cutting-edge  imaging  techniques  across  positive  and  negative
reinforcement  or  throughout  the  development  and  expression  of  SUD.  My  lab  will  determine  the  longitudinal
encoding of individual neurons using 2-photon imaging with microprisms to record hundreds of tracked neurons9
and determine the in-vivo coupling of the LHA and downstream DA signaling using multi-site fiber photometry
during  behavior.  I  will  employe  the  head-fixed  procedures  I  have  developed  to  model  positive  and  negative
reinfrocement10  along  with  head-fixed  intravenous  self-administration  to  model  SUD11,12.  I  will  utilize
computational  approaches  including  general  linear  models  and  others  to  determine  the  neural  encoding  of
reinforcement.  I  hypothesize  that  subsets  of  LHA  neurons  exhibit  distinct  encoding  of  positive  and  negative
reinforcement  that  selectively  regulates  DA  release  in  specific  downstream  areas  to  motivate  drug  taking.
Untangling the neuronal encoding of positive and negative reinforcement will inform therapeutic approaches that
can selectively shape these mechanisms. Some of the projects outlined here are supported by a NIDA K99/R00.
Project 1: LHA and striatal DA encoding and function in positive and negative reinforcement. The LHA
contains GABA and Glut neurons that drive opposing reinforcement phenotypes and regulation of dopamine in
the ventral striatum3,13–15, but the role of these populations during positive/negative reinforcement remains poorly
understood.  I  will  determine  the  dynamics  of  tracked  neurons  using  2-photon  imaging  and  the  relation  to
downstream DA signaling using multi-site fiber photometry. I will then test the causal role of this circuit using
chemogenetics and disconnect approaches. These project will establish a framework for how the LHA regulates
the DA system to mediate positive/negative reinforcement that may be recruited during the development of SUD.
Project 2: LHA and striatal DA encoding during development and expression of motivation for opioids.
The role of DA in SUD is well established but the spatiotemporal DA dynamics during opioid self-administration
and the role of descending LHA regulation of the DA system in SUD remains  poorly understood. I will utilize
approaches outlined in Project 1 to determine how this circuit regulates motivation for opioids.
Project 3: Linking activity-defined clusters of LHA neurons to circuit and behavior. It is unknown if there
are discrete clusters of neurons in the LHA that selectively modulate subcomponents of behavior and DA release
in subregions of the striatum. I will use 2-photon imaging during reinforcement behavior to identify clusters of
cells  that  show  unique  activity  dynamics  and  then  measure  the  behavioral  function  of  these  cells  using
holographic stimulation9 during behavior. I will also assess the relationship between these cells and downstream
DA by multiplexing 2-photon imaging and multi-site fiber photometry.
Past  Research  Accomplishments:  Postdoctoral  Research:  I  developed  a  novel  head-fixed  system  for
measuring a range of consumption and reinforcement behaviors in head-fixed mice10, and used this system to
assess the role the LHA and DA release in consumption (in preparation). I conducted an exhaustive review of
the signaling and function of subcortical GABA and Glutamate in reinforcement / valence16. I have also shared
my technical expertise in designing and implementing head-fixed approaches as a co-author a primer in Neuron17
and a protocols paper on head-fixed IVSA (in preparation)
